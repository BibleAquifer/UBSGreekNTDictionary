## ὑακίνθινος (id: 005082000000000)

ὑακίνθινος
==========

ὑακίνθινος (G5191)

adjc. \| \-η, \-ον \| ὑάκινθος

79\.37 · Features of Objects · Color

blue pertaining to being the color of the flower hyacinth (see [2\.41](#2.41)), probably a shade of blue Since in some languages the flower hyacinth may not be as well known as precious stones such as sapphire and turquoise, it may be possible to render [REV 9:17](https://ref.ly/Jude9:17) as ‘blue as sapphire’ or ‘blue as turquoise.’ The justification for this is simply that the Greek term ὑακίνθινος had become primarily a designation for color.

## ὕδωρ (id: 005095000000000)

ὕδωρ
====

ὕδωρ (G5204)

noun, n. \| ὕδατος \| ἄνυδρος · ὑγρός · ὑδρία · ὑδροποτέω · ὑδρωπικός · ὑετός

2\.7 · Natural Substances · Water

water a clear liquid that is without color, taste, or smell in its pure form; it forms rivers, lakes, and oceans, and sustains life on earth In [2PE 3:5](https://ref.ly/1Pet3:5) the Greek expression is admittedly both strange and obscure. ἐξ ὕδατος presumably refers to the fact that the land emerged from the water, or at least was separated from the water. The phrase δἰ ὕδατος, literally ‘through water,’ may be locative, that is to say, it may refer to a place, but it is more likely to have some reference to water as an instrument which contributed to the act of producing the heavens and earth. However, the active instrument for this creation was τῷ τοῦ θεοῦ λόγῳ ‘the word of God.’. Since the dative case ὕδατι ‘by water’ is clearly instrumental in verse 6, it may be that ἐξ ὕδατος καὶ δἰ ὕδατος in verse 5 refers in a spacial sense to the formation of the heavens and the dry land ‘from water and through a watery area.’  
Even more complex, however, than the interpretation of ‘water’ in [2PE 3:5](https://ref.ly/1Pet3:5) is the fact that in a number of languages there are quite different terms for ὕδωρ depending upon the type of water, its location, and its function. For example, water in some kind of bowl or container (and thus most likely used for drinking or cooking) may be referred to by one term, while water in a lake, stream, or ocean may be identified by quite a different term. Similarly, an important distinction may be made between salt water and fresh water, and an even further distinction may be introduced in the case of brackish water, that is to say, water which is partially salty. Some languages make a distinction between water which collects in pools and water which is flowing in streams or rivers. Accordingly, careful attention must be paid to contexts in order to select the appropriate term for ὕδωρ (see also [8\.64](#8.64) on [JHN 19:34](https://ref.ly/Luke19:34)).

## ὑετός (id: 005096000000000)

ὑετός
=====

ὑετός (G5205)

noun, m. \| \-οῦ \| ὕδωρ

2\.10 · Natural Substances · Water

rain, rain water water that falls in drops condensed from water vapor in the atmosphere Though ὑετός, which means rain as a substance (for ‘the event of raining,’ see [14\.10](#14.10)), generally occurs in contexts in which the focus is upon the ‘the event of raining,’ as supplied by a verb (for example, [ACT 14:17](https://ref.ly/John14:17); [JAS 5:18](https://ref.ly/Heb5:18); and [REV 11:6](https://ref.ly/Jude11:6)), in [HEB 6:7](https://ref.ly/Phlm6:7) the focus is clearly upon the substance. Though the Greek text of [HEB 6:7](https://ref.ly/Phlm6:7) speaks literally of the ground ‘drinking the rain,’ this figurative expression can rarely be reproduced. Sometimes one can use an expression such as ‘soaks up,’ the type of term which might be employed in speaking of a sponge soaking up moisture, but in a number of instances one can only speak of ‘the rain disappearing into the earth’ or ‘the earth causing the rain to disappear into it.’  
Translators may be confronted with several problems in selecting an appropriate term for ‘rain,’ since some languages make important distinctions in terms for ‘rain’ depending upon the season of the year and the quantity of rain. For example, rain falling in the dry season may be called by quite a different term from that which falls in the rainy season. Similarly, a series of distinctions may be made in terms for rain depending upon whether one is speaking of a torrential rainstorm or an average rain or a continuous mist.

[ACT 14:17](https://ref.ly/John14:17) · [HEB 6:7](https://ref.ly/Phlm6:7) · [JAS 5:18](https://ref.ly/Heb5:18) · [REV 11:6](https://ref.ly/Jude11:6)

14\.10 · Physical Events and States · Rain

rain, to rain rain, whether light or torrential Though most languages have a general term for rain, the event of raining is more frequently referred to by a verb rather than by a noun, since rain is something which takes place and is not regarded as a thing or mass, unless, of course, one is referring to the water which comes down as rain, that is to say, ‘rain water’ (see [2\.10](#2.10)). In some languages the event of raining is generally expressed idiomatically as ‘God is urinating.’ This might appear unduly crude and anthropomorphic, but to the speakers of such a language the expression is often quite acceptable and has no undesirable connotations.  
Though in English one may use an impersonal subject with the verb ‘to rain,’ for example, ‘it is raining,’ many languages require some kind of personal agent, for example, ‘God is raining,’ or an impersonal agent such as ‘the sky is raining’ or ‘the clouds are raining.’

[ACT 14:17](https://ref.ly/John14:17) · [ACT 28:2](https://ref.ly/John28:2) · [JAS 5:18](https://ref.ly/Heb5:18)

## ὕλη (id: 005099000000000)

ὕλη
===

ὕλη (G5208)

noun, f. \| \-ης \| διϋλίζω

3\.3 · Plants · Trees

forest a dense growth of trees covering a relatively large area In [JAS 3:5](https://ref.ly/Heb3:5) it is also possible to interpret ὕλη as wood which has been cut up, either firewood or wood used for construction (see ὕλη\[b] wood, pile of wood, [3\.64](#3.64)).  
In some languages spoken by people living in tropical forest areas the equivalent of ‘forest’ is simply the expression ‘where no one lives,’ based upon the fact that people live in clearings and everything else is forest. Such a rendering, however, may not lead to a satisfactory translation of [JAS 3:5](https://ref.ly/Heb3:5) (the only occurrence of ὕλη in the NT), in which case one is better advised to interpret ὕλη in the sense of ‘pile of wood’ ([3\.64](#3.64)).

wood, pile of wood the woody part of trees or bushes In [JAS 3:5](https://ref.ly/Heb3:5) ὕλη may be interpreted as a forest (see [3\.3](#3.3)).  
As already noted in the case of ξύλον\[a] ([3\.60](#3.60)), a number of languages make a distinction between wood used as building material and wood employed in heating or cooking.

## ὑπηρέτης (id: 005146000000000)

ὑπηρέτης
========

ὑπηρέτης (G5257)

noun, m. \| \-ου \| ὑπηρετέω

35\.20 · Help, Care For · Serve

servant (derivative of ὑπηρετέω\[a] ‘to serve,’ [35\.19](#35.19)) a person who renders service In rendering θεράπων, ὑπηρέτης, and διάκονος\[a] in the sense of ‘servant,’ it is important to avoid a term which would be too specific, for example, ‘one who serves meals’ or ‘one who works around the house.’ It may, in fact, be necessary to use an expression which means essentially ‘helper.’  
In the NT ὑπηρέτης is employed to refer to many diverse types of servants, such as attendants to a king, officers of the Sanhedrin, attendants of magistrates, and, especially in the Gospel of John, Jewish Temple guards.
